## How I Read LinkedIn Like a Robot Without Looking Like One

This is the non-engineer version. No code. If you've ever wondered how someone pulls data out of LinkedIn at scale without their account getting banned every five minutes, this is the explanation.

## Start with the problem

LinkedIn has data you want. Names, jobs, companies, who works where, who knows who, who recently changed roles. You can read it for free in a browser. But the moment you try to read a lot of it (say, ten thousand profiles a day) LinkedIn locks you out. Captchas, redirects, a banned account, sometimes a permanently dead login.

Why? Because reading ten thousand profiles a day is not a thing humans do. LinkedIn is constantly trying to tell the difference between a person scrolling around and a script pretending to be one. The whole game of scraping is convincing them you're a person.

## What "looking like a person" actually means

When you open LinkedIn in Chrome, your browser sends hundreds of tiny signals with every request. Most of them you've never heard of:

- What version of Chrome you're running (down to the patch number)
- Your screen size and how you've resized the window
- Your IP address, and what kind of network it's on (home wifi vs a data centre)
- The exact order your browser shakes hands with LinkedIn's servers (this is called TLS, and every browser does it slightly differently)
- Whether you scrolled before clicking
- How fast you moved the mouse
- Tracking cookies that LinkedIn's own anti-bot script writes into your browser after watching you behave like a person for a few seconds

A naive scraper gets caught because it gets one or two of these right and a hundred of them wrong. The IP says "data centre in Virginia", the browser fingerprint says "Chrome 91", the cookies say "never been here before", the timing says "two hundred profile reads in thirty seconds". No human matches that profile.

## Why one account is never enough

Even if you make one scraper that looks perfectly human, you still have a problem: a single LinkedIn account that reads a thousand profiles a day eventually trips a rate limit. So you need many accounts.

Now the harder problem appears. If you have twenty accounts and they all look identical (same browser version, same screen size, same IP range, same rhythm of clicks) LinkedIn doesn't see twenty different people. It sees one bot pretending to be twenty people. The moment they ban one, they ban them all.

The whole architecture of this project is built around one idea: **each fake account must look like a completely different real person on a completely different real computer**. None of them can share anything. They cannot share an IP. They cannot share a browser version. They cannot share a timing pattern. If you do this right, twenty accounts look like twenty unrelated humans. If you do it wrong, twenty accounts look like a server farm.

## How to make twenty accounts look unrelated

For each LinkedIn account I want to use, I give it its own private bundle of stuff that never gets reused:

1. **Its own IP address.** Not a data centre IP. A residential proxy, which is basically an internet connection in someone's actual house. Each account keeps the same residential IP for its whole life, so it looks like a real person logging in from home.
2. **Its own browser identity.** A specific version of Chrome, a specific screen size, a specific set of fonts and capabilities. Picked from a pool so the twenty accounts spread evenly across, say, four different Chrome versions and five different screen sizes. Twenty unrelated home computers.
3. **Its own folder of cookies and session data on my hard drive.** Every account has its own browser profile, never mixed with the others.
4. **Its own pacing.** How many seconds between each read, what time of day it reads, how often it rests. Each account has its own rhythm.

When LinkedIn looks at any single request, all the signals match each other ("yes this looks like Sarah from Manchester on her home computer"). When LinkedIn looks at all twenty accounts side by side, nothing connects them.

## The two-step warmup

You can't just hand LinkedIn a brand new fake account and start reading profiles. They watch new accounts closely. So every account gets warmed up first, in two stages.

**Stage one is fast.** I make a few quiet, normal-looking requests to LinkedIn from the new account, the kind of requests a browser makes when it first loads the homepage. LinkedIn sends back some session cookies. I catch them. If LinkedIn responds with anything weird (a redirect to login, a checkpoint screen, a sign that the account is already burned) I throw the account out. It will never work, no point trying.

**Stage two is slower.** I open a real Chrome browser (controlled by a script) and load the LinkedIn feed. The script makes the mouse jiggle a bit, scrolls down, waits a few seconds. This is for LinkedIn's anti-bot script, which sits inside the page and watches you behave for a moment before deciding you're real. After about twenty seconds, it decides you're a human and silently writes some tracking cookies into the browser. I save those cookies. The browser closes.

Now the account is "warm". From this point on, I don't need to open a real browser for it. I have all the cookies and session information a real browser would have built up, and I can just send requests over the network pretending to be that browser.

There's one rule that I will never break: I never warm up the same account twice in the same week. Doing that is the single loudest signal of a stolen cookie being replayed by a fraudster, and LinkedIn pattern-matches on it directly.

## How a search actually runs

Once I have a pool of warm accounts, I can ask the system: "find me marketing directors in London."

The system doesn't pick a random account. It runs through a small checklist:

- Which accounts are warm and healthy right now?
- Of those, which haven't hit their daily read limit?
- Of those, which have had a long enough break since their last read?
- Of those, which has been used least recently?

That last one is the winner. The system picks it, reserves it (so two parallel searches don't grab the same account at the same time), and makes the read. If it works, the account's daily counter ticks up by one and it goes back into the pool. The whole search takes a couple of seconds and the user never has to know which fake account ran it.

If LinkedIn pushes back ("hey, you look a bit suspicious"), the system has a graceful response for every kind of pushback:

- A mild "are you sure?" gets a brief browser visit to refresh the human-detection cookies, then the account is back to normal.
- A "your session is dead" means that account is gone forever. The system writes it off and the search retries on a different one.
- A "you're going too fast" means the daily limit was set too high. Tune it down.

The point is: any single account can break and the system keeps working. No one read ever depends on one specific account.

## Why this matters beyond LinkedIn

The pattern here isn't really about LinkedIn. It's about the general shape of any system that has to look like many independent humans:

- **Identity is a bundle, not a single thing.** A real person is an IP, a browser, a rhythm, a history, a set of cookies. If any one of these is shared across accounts, the accounts are linked. If none of them are, the accounts look unrelated.
- **The expensive thing (a real browser) is a guest, not a resident.** Running twenty Chromes all day would melt my laptop and produce a thousand subtle inconsistencies. So Chrome only shows up for the moments where nothing else will do: the initial warmup, and the occasional refresh when a tracking cookie expires.
- **Constant rhythms are a fingerprint.** A bot that reads exactly every five seconds is more suspicious than one that reads slowly, then fast, then slowly again. Humans are uneven. Bots should be uneven too.
- **Pushback is a signal, not a failure.** Most of the time, LinkedIn isn't saying "you're banned forever". It's saying "prove you're still a human", and the answer is to briefly open a real browser, let their detection script see a person, and carry on.

## The honest caveat

This whole project only works on accounts I am willing to lose. Some of them will eventually get burned, no matter how careful the setup is. That's fine. The architecture assumes accounts will die and the fleet keeps working around them.

I would never run this against a LinkedIn account I actually use. Neither should you.
